## Why snow is hard

A huge share of the world's fresh water is stored as seasonal snow, and how much water a snowpack holds — its **snow water equivalent** — drives water supply for billions of people. Yet it's one of the hardest things to measure from space. Microwaves interact with snow in a complicated way: they scatter off the countless ice grains inside the pack, and that scattering depends on grain size, layering, and wetness in ways that are easy to get wrong.

## What I work on

Two complementary lines. First, more than a decade of **physics-based snow modeling** — describing how radar signals scatter inside a snowpack so that a measurement can be turned into snow water equivalent. Second, as **JPL-PI** of a NASA Terrestrial Hydrology project, a newer approach that uses **InSAR**: comparing the phase of radar signals across repeat satellite passes to track how the snow changes, and assimilating multiple radar frequencies together to pin down the answer more tightly.
